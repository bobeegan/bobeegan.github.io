Recent NASA Projects

The James Webb Space Telescope (pictured left) and DART (pictured right) programs are NASA's two of NASA's new projects. The Webb will allow us to see more of our universe than ever before with cameras and sensors which far outpace the Hubble's. The DART spacecraft is a test craft designed to asses our ability to redirect a potential Earth threatening asteroid.
All information on this page comes from NASA's website.

James Webb Space Telescope

The James Webb Space Telescope was launched from French Guiana on Christmas of 2021, and the first set of images it took was released to the public by NASA on July 11th, 2022.
Webb's primary imaging device is the Near Infrared Camera (NIRCam). NIRCam is capable of detetecting light from the earliest stars and galaxies in the process of formation, allowing us to peer backwards in time and distantly into the universe to their creation. It can also detect light from younger and closer stars and galaxies- like younger stars in the Milky Way.
NIRCam also has coronagraphs which will allow us to see planets orbiting nearby stars by blocking the light from brighter objects, just like shielding your eyes from the sun can allow you to see better.
The Webb also has a spectrograph which will allow scientists to examine light being filtered through the atmospheres of exoplanets- and therefore learn about their chemical compositions.

Double Asteroid Redirection Test

The DART spacecraft successfully impacted with an asteroid on September 26th, 2022 as a test to determine our ability to alter an asteroid's course.
DART impacted with the Didymos binary asteroid system, made up of asteroids Didymos and Dimorphos. Specifically, DART hit Dimorphos, the smaller asteroid of the two. On impact, Dimorphos generated a dust trail similar to the ones generated by comets and large chunks broke off of it.
NASA scientists estimate that this impact shortened the orbit of the Didymos system by 1%, or about 10 minutes. This information allows scientists to understand how impacting asteroids alters their courses, so that if the Earth is threatened by one in the future, we will be able to calculate how much force must be generated to protect our planet.
